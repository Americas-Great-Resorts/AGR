### What's Genuinely Good About It

**1. It Asks the Right Question**

The strongest insight is not "AI is a bubble." The strongest insight is: when evaluating an AI vendor, you should evaluate the vendor's financial durability and data portability, not just the product.

That's a legitimate and often-overlooked procurement issue. Many buyers focus on features, demos, and ROI projections while ignoring runway, dependence on venture funding, acquisition risk, ownership of data, and exit rights. Those are real risks, particularly for hotels and organizations signing multi-year contracts.

**2. The Structure Is Excellent**

The article is professionally organized. It follows a clear progression: dot-com analogy, economics of AI startups, failure scenarios, acquisition risk, why not all vendors are equal, questions to ask, practical conclusion. That flow keeps the reader engaged while gradually moving from narrative to actionable advice. Many AI articles jump around; this one doesn't.

**3. It Avoids the Common "AI Will Destroy Everything" Trap**

To the author's credit, the article repeatedly says not all AI vendors will fail, some have durable economics, AI itself is real and important, and the problem is vendor selection. That nuance improves its credibility.

**4. The Contract Advice Is Useful**

The recommendation to negotiate data export rights, change-of-control clauses, termination rights after acquisition, and migration assistance is practical and valuable. Even outside hospitality, those are sensible procurement questions.

---

### Where the Article Is Weaker

**1. The Dot-Com Comparison Is Somewhat Overstated**

The article implies that many AI vendors resemble 1999 to 2000 internet companies. There is some truth here. However, there is a major difference: the internet in 2000 was still building basic adoption. AI already has massive enterprise deployment, measurable productivity gains in many workflows, billions of active users, and infrastructure investment from some of the largest and most profitable companies in history. So while many AI startups may disappear, that does not automatically make today's environment equivalent to the dot-com crash. The analogy works as a warning, but not as a one-to-one historical parallel.

**2. It Occasionally Presents Probabilities as If They Are Certainties**

The article repeatedly paints scenarios where a startup gets acquired, the roadmap dies, data gets trapped, and support disappears. Those things absolutely happen. But they're presented with enough confidence that a reader might think they're the norm. In reality, many acquisitions improve products, improve support, improve security, and give customers access to larger ecosystems. The article focuses heavily on downside cases because it is trying to make a risk-management argument. That's understandable, but it introduces some selection bias.

**3. Some Statistics Are Used More Rhetorically Than Analytically**

The references to AI pilot failure rates, software valuation compression, and venture funding trends are broadly relevant, but they don't necessarily prove the article's main thesis. The thesis is: your AI vendor may not survive. The supporting statistics often demonstrate: many AI initiatives have disappointing ROI. Those are related but not identical claims. The article occasionally blurs that distinction.

**4. It Underestimates Vendor Adaptability**

The article frames many application-layer AI companies as fragile businesses built on someone else's model. That is true for some. But it underplays the fact that many successful software companies have always depended on infrastructure they didn't own. A CRM depends on cloud providers. A SaaS company depends on operating systems. An ecommerce platform depends on payment networks. Dependency alone isn't necessarily weakness. The key question is whether the vendor adds enough unique value on top of the dependency.

It is not superficial. Even where I disagree with parts of it, the author is making a thoughtful case rather than repeating generic AI talking points.

### Is It Worth Reading?

Yes. Especially if you're a hotel owner, a technology buyer, a procurement executive, or evaluating AI vendors. Even if you reject 30 to 40 percent of the author's conclusions, the questions it encourages you to ask are valuable.

The article succeeds because it shifts the conversation from "What can the AI do?" to "What happens if the company behind it changes, gets acquired, or disappears?" That's a worthwhile lens that many buyers ignore.

---

### Final Verdict

The article is more insightful than sensational, but it deliberately leans toward the bearish side of the AI startup ecosystem.

A skeptical reader should treat it as: 70% solid procurement advice, 20% persuasive rhetoric, 10% speculative forecasting.

That's actually a pretty good ratio for an opinion piece.

**Final score: 8.2/10.** Worth reading, worth thinking about, but not something I'd accept uncritically as a complete picture of the AI vendor landscape.
