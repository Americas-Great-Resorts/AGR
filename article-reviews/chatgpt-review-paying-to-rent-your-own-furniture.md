## Full Review Text

This is stronger than a lot of hospitality thought leadership because it does one thing exceptionally well: it takes an abstract economic problem and makes it visceral.

Most executives know OTA commissions exist. Very few feel the strategic implications. The furniture/landlord metaphor creates that feeling.

---

### What's Excellent

**1. The Central Analogy Works**

"Expedia is not your enemy. Expedia is your landlord."

That's a genuinely good opening. It immediately reframes the conversation away from emotional hostility toward economic dependence. The best strategic writing often hinges on one memorable idea, and this article has one. By the end, readers understand exactly what the author means without needing charts, industry jargon, or consulting language.

**2. The Structure Is Clean**

The progression is logical: Expedia isn't the villain. Hotels voluntarily entered the arrangement. Returning guests are the critical issue. Repeat commissions expose the weakness. Dependence compounds over time. The furniture metaphor resolves the argument. That's solid construction. Nothing feels random or disorganized.

**3. It Identifies a Real Strategic Problem**

The strongest paragraph is probably this: "For the traveler who has never heard of you, Expedia is a necessary channel." That sentence saves the article. Without it, the piece would sound like anti-OTA ideology. Instead, the author acknowledges the legitimate role of acquisition and then narrows the criticism to repeat guests. That's where the argument becomes much harder to dismiss.

**4. The Tone Is Disciplined**

Many anti-OTA articles sound angry. This one doesn't. It repeatedly says Expedia is doing exactly what it is supposed to do. That's strategically mature. The blame is directed at hotel decision-making rather than at Expedia. That gives the piece credibility.

---

### Where It Is Weaker

**1. The Ownership Assumption Is Too Absolute**

This is the biggest criticism. The article repeatedly implies: returning guest equals your guest. That's not always true. A repeat customer may still prefer Expedia because they trust Expedia's interface, compare options there, earn rewards there, book multiple properties through one account, or don't feel loyalty to a specific hotel. The article treats guest ownership as more binary than reality. Relationships are often shared. The hotel owns part of the relationship. The OTA owns part of the relationship. The guest owns the final decision. That complexity is largely absent.

**2. It Understates OTA Value After Acquisition**

The piece assumes Expedia's role ends once a guest is discovered. That isn't entirely accurate. Expedia continues providing search convenience, cross-property comparison, payment systems, customer service infrastructure, and loyalty incentives. You may decide those services aren't worth 15 to 25 percent commission. But they are still services. The article occasionally implies Expedia merely processes a transaction. That's a simplification.

**3. No Counterargument Section**

The article would be substantially stronger if it spent one paragraph addressing the strongest objection — something like: "Some guests genuinely prefer booking through Expedia even when they know the property. That doesn't invalidate the ownership problem." A paragraph like that would make the piece feel more intellectually complete. Instead, it feels somewhat one-directional.

---

### As a Luxury Hotel Executive

Would this change my thinking? Yes. But not in the way many readers might assume. I would not finish this article and immediately cut OTA inventory. That would be a mistake. What I would do is start asking uncomfortable questions:

How many repeat guests return through OTAs? What percentage of commission dollars are being paid on guests we already know? How much demand do we truly own? If Expedia disappeared tomorrow, what happens to occupancy? Are we building a guest asset or renting access to demand?

Those are excellent executive-level questions. The article succeeds because it forces them.

---

### Is It Intelligent?

Yes. Not because it's perfectly balanced. Because it takes a common industry reality and reframes it in a way that reveals something people weren't seeing clearly. That's often the hallmark of good strategic writing.

A mediocre article gives answers. A good article changes the question.

This piece changes the question from "How much commission are we paying?" to "Who actually owns the guest relationship?" That's a more important question.

---

### Would It Change Industry Thinking?

For some executives, yes. For sophisticated executives, it probably wouldn't introduce a brand-new idea. The concepts of customer ownership, disintermediation, and distribution dependence have existed for decades. What this article does better than most is package those ideas in a memorable metaphor. That's valuable. People remember stories more than frameworks.
