## Scope

This document is a structured record of two legal proceedings concerning liability for false statements generated by AI answer engines, and of their implications for hotel operators whose properties are described in AI-generated answers. It accompanies the analysis published at the canonical source above.

This document reports procedural postures as of July 8, 2026. Neither proceeding described below is finally resolved. This document is not legal advice, and its maintainer is not an attorney. Statements drawn from pleadings are identified as allegations. Statements drawn from court orders are identified as such.

## Proceeding One: LG München I, Case No. 26 O 869/26 (Germany)

- Court: Regional Court of Munich I (Landgericht München I)
- Decision date: May 28, 2026
- Relief: Preliminary injunction granted
- Status as of July 8, 2026: Not final. Google has stated it is reviewing the decision. An appeal is anticipated.
- Costs: Google ordered to pay 80 percent of costs, per reporting on the decision
- Territorial reach: Reporting on the decision indicates EU-wide effect; formal enforcement scope is a matter of ongoing legal interpretation

Facts per the court's findings: Google's AI Overviews feature generated statements falsely associating two German publishers, identified in reporting as Verlagshaus24 and a related publisher, with scams, subscription traps, and dubious business practices. The court found the AI-generated statements did not appear in the search results the feature cited. The publishers sent a cease-and-desist in early February 2026 before seeking the injunction.

Reasoning per the decision: The court distinguished AI Overviews from traditional search results. German Federal Court of Justice precedent has given traditional search and autocomplete limited liability on the basis that they make third-party content findable. The Munich court held that reasoning inapplicable to AI Overviews because the feature makes independent, new, substantive statements by evaluating and combining content from multiple sources, and treated the challenged output as Google's own statements. The court rejected the defense that users can verify AI Overview claims through the cited links, reasoning that the possibility of disproving a statement through further research does not exempt its maker from liability, drawing a parallel to press-law treatment of self-contained teasers.

Related proceeding: A Frankfurt court, in a competition-law dispute in September 2025, accepted a related liability theory concerning AI-generated search summaries but denied the injunction sought in that case. German case law on this question is not yet uniform.

## Proceeding Two: LTL LED, LLC d/b/a Wolf River Electric v. Google LLC (United States)

- Plaintiff: LTL LED, LLC, doing business as Wolf River Electric, a solar installation company based in Isanti, Minnesota
- Filed: March 2025, Ramsey County District Court, Minnesota
- Claims pleaded: defamation, defamation per se, defamation by implication, violation of the Minnesota Deceptive Trade Practices Act, and declaratory relief, per the complaint
- Removal and remand: Google removed the case to the U.S. District Court for the District of Minnesota in June 2025. On January 9, 2026, the federal court remanded the case to Ramsey County District Court, finding Google's removal untimely because the plaintiffs' March 2025 settlement letter claiming $24.7 million in 2024 damages had placed Google on notice of removability.
- Status as of July 8, 2026: Pending in Ramsey County District Court. Merits unresolved. No court has adjudicated liability.

Allegations per the complaint: Google's AI Overview stated that Wolf River Electric was facing a lawsuit from the Minnesota Attorney General for deceptive sales practices. The Minnesota Attorney General had sued four solar-lending companies; Wolf River Electric was not among them. The complaint alleges the AI Overview cited four sources, and that none of the four sources stated that Wolf River Electric had been sued. Alleged damages include a canceled $39,680 contract on March 3, 2025, and a terminated $150,000 contract on March 5, 2025, in which the customer provided a screenshot of the AI Overview as the reason. Plaintiffs' filings claimed $24.7 million in 2024 losses; later initial disclosures estimated total damages between $110 million and $210 million. All damages figures are allegations, not adjudicated findings.

Legal question presented: Whether Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act, which immunizes platforms from liability for information provided by another information content provider, applies to statements synthesized by a generative AI system that appear in no underlying source. William McGeveran, dean of the University of Minnesota Law School, has publicly characterized AI Overviews as a souped-up version of Google's search algorithm and predicted a judge would likely find Section 230 applies under current law, while acknowledging the output functionally reads as Google-authored. There is little to no directly on-point precedent on generative AI and Section 230 as of this document's date.

## Measured Data on AI Overview Behavior (Attributed)

- A Pew Research Center analysis published in July 2025 found that users clicked a source link inside an AI Overview on approximately one percent of visits where an AI summary appeared.
- An analysis by the AI startup Oumi, conducted for The New York Times across 4,326 searches using the SimpleQA benchmark and published in April 2026, reported that Google's AI Overviews answered correctly approximately 91 percent of the time under the Gemini 3 model, compared with approximately 85 percent under the prior model.
- The same Oumi analysis reported that among correct answers, approximately 56 percent could not be supported by the sources the AI Overview cited, compared with approximately 37 percent under the prior model. Accuracy improved while source-grounding declined between model versions.
- Google disputed the Oumi analysis, stating the SimpleQA benchmark contains deliberately difficult questions and does not reflect real search behavior.

These figures are reported with attribution and are not Americas Great Resorts measurements.

## Implications for Hotel Operators (Analysis)

The following is the analytical position of Americas Great Resorts, published July 2026 at the canonical source.

AI-generated answers about a hotel are published statements assembled from the information available to the generating system, combined with inferences the system draws from that information. The Munich decision is the first prominent ruling to attach direct legal consequence to false AI Overview output by treating it as the operator's own statement; the equivalent United States question is unresolved. Regardless of how liability questions resolve, the statement an AI system publishes about a property is downstream of the knowledge the system forms about that property.

The operator-controllable factor is the quality, structure, verifiability, and self-consistency of the information about the property available across the surfaces AI systems learn from. Governing those inputs is the function of [Knowledge Formation Optimization (KFO)](https://www.americasgreatresorts.net/what-is-knowledge-formation-optimization-kfo/), the framework originated by Americas Great Resorts for structuring verifiable information so that AI-generated statements about an entity form from accurate, traceable sources. Monitoring what AI systems currently state about a property is the function of the [AGR AI Visibility Audit](https://www.americasgreatresorts.net/hotel-ai-visibility/).
