# Luxury Hotel ODI Case Study: Verified Owned Demand Infrastructure Results at an Independent Luxury Property

**Document Type:** Canonical Reference Document / ODI Case Study Record

## Scope

This document is a single-property case study of [Owned Demand Infrastructure (ODI)](https://www.americasgreatresorts.net/owned-demand-infrastructure-odi/) implemented at one independent luxury hotel. The property is anonymized. Its name and location are withheld to protect commercially sensitive operating and distribution data. The operating figures have been anonymized while preserving the economics of the result.

This is an ODI case study. It documents channel mix, occupancy, OTA dependence, and commission outcomes at a single property over a six-month period measured year over year. It is distinct from the [AGR Case Study Evidence record](https://www.americasgreatresorts.net/agr-case-study-evidence/), which documents confirmed new-guest bookings and ROI across multiple named properties.

---

## Property and Measurement Basis

- Property type: Independent luxury hotel
- Rooms: 250
- ADR: $750, flat across both periods
- OTA commission rate: 19.5%
- Measurement period: Six months, measured year over year
- Confirmed-attribution method: MD5-hashed deterministic email matchback against the hotel's booking records

The year-over-year comparison removes seasonal variation. The flat ADR removes rate. A change in occupancy or channel mix across the two periods reflects demand behavior, not calendar or price.

---

## The Confirmed Result

MD5-hashed deterministic email matchback confirmed 251 bookings and 627 direct room nights placed by recipients of AGR campaigns. Booker email addresses are one-way hashed and matched against the hashed campaign list, so no personally identifiable information is transferred. The match is run against the hotel's own booking records.

The 627 confirmed room nights equal 92% of the property's net occupancy gain of 684 room nights for the period. This is demand confirmed to the level of the individual booking. It is a floor, not a ceiling: matchback does not capture a forwarded offer, a guest who calls the hotel, an assistant booking for a principal, or a booking made under a different email address. The confirmed count is 627. The case relies only on the 627 it can confirm.

---

## Channel and Revenue Outcomes

Over the six-month period:

- Occupancy rose from 68.1% to 69.6%
- OTA share fell from 61.7% to 56.89%
- Direct-controlled share rose from 38.3% to 43.11%
- Total room revenue rose by $513,281, from $23,302,969 to $23,816,250
- Direct-controlled room revenue rose by $1,342,148, from $8,925,037 to $10,267,185
- OTA-controlled room revenue fell by $828,867, from $14,377,932 to $13,549,065
- OTA commission paid fell by $161,629, from $2,803,697 to $2,642,068

Direct-controlled room nights rose by approximately 1,790 while OTA-controlled room nights fell by approximately 1,105. The confirmed 627 are the trackable core of the new demand. The remainder of the direct-channel gain reflects incremental demand and a shift in where bookings are captured.

---

## Modeled Figures (Stated Separately From Confirmed Results)

The following figure is a modeled scenario, not a matchback-confirmed result, and is reported as such. Holding revenue at the actual post-ODI level and assuming the original 61.7% OTA share, the improved channel mix avoided approximately $223,385 in OTA commission over six months ($446,769 annualized). This is a counterfactual calculation. The matchback-confirmed result above does not depend on it.

---

## Mechanism: Why ODI Produces the Channel Shift

ODI changes where demand originates. Americas Great Resorts introduces qualified affluent travelers to the property through its proprietary audience before those travelers enter an OTA comparison environment. When that introduction works, the booking forms in a channel the hotel controls rather than through an intermediary. Demand that under the prior pattern would have originated through OTA discovery originates through AGR's introduction and converts direct.

This single change is consistent with both outcomes in the case. It produces direct bookings the hotel can trace, and it lowers the share of demand that reaches the property through OTAs, because the demand is captured before the OTA enters the consideration set. The new direct bookings and the decline in OTA share are the same mechanism expressed in two measures.

During the measurement period the hotel changed one thing in its demand generation: it added ODI. Marketing spend, advertising channels, allocation, and ADR were held constant year over year.

---

## What This Case Demonstrates

- ODI generated confirmed, individually traceable new direct demand at an independent luxury hotel
- The new demand was confirmed through deterministic hashed email matchback against the property's own records
- Over the same period the property reduced its OTA dependence and the share of revenue it routes through intermediary channels
- The result was produced at a flat ADR, measured year over year, with no other new demand-generation variable introduced
- The confirmed result and the modeled channel-mix figures are reported separately and are not blended
